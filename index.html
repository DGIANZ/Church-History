    81 AD

  • Death of the Apostle John

    The death of the Apostle John ushered in a new era. Without living witnesses to Jesus Christ, the Church could no longer seek them out to settle disputes. A felt need for a canon emerged.

    90 AD

  • 1 Clement Composed

    Clement of Rome writes to the Corinth church. In the letter, he offers a solution to the disunity of the truth. He stressed obedience to the bishop.

    95 AD

  • Domitian Dies

    Emperor Domitian dies, and the persecution against Christians comes to an end.

    96 AD

  • Trajan Becomes Emperor

    Trajan becomes the Roman emperor. He initiates persecution against Christians.

    98 AD

  • Apostolic Fathers & Apologists Arise

    The Second Century saw the rise of the Apostolic Fathers and Apologists. With the death of the Apostle John, early Christians lost their ability to seek out eyewitnesses to settle disputes. Thus, the Apostolic Fathers rose to prominence. These men were disciples of eyewitnesses of Jesus Christ or the disciple of a disciple who was discipled by an eyewitness and thus regarded as authoritative. Examples include Clement of Rome, Polycarp of Smyrna, and Ignatius of Antioch. Authoritative writings of this period are also referred to as Apostolic Fathers because of their influence among Christians during this period. Examples are The Didache, Epistle of Barnabas, Shepherd of Hermas, etc.

    The apologists emerged as defenders of Christianity against misunderstandings and persecution. They began using Greek philosophy to defend the Gospel, making it appear rational. Examples include Aristides, Justin Martyr, Melito of Sardis, Athenagoras of Athens, Tatian, and Theophilus of Antioch. These men utilized Greek philosophy and reason to reach the Romans. Their contributions led Christianity to become mainstream rather than fading into a folk religion. Nevertheless, some early Christians opposed such use of Greek philosophy, fearing that it would corrupt the message of salvation.

    2nd Century

  • Second Century Heresies

    The second century saw the emergence of Gnosticism and Montanism. The Gnostics taught that the cosmos is incurable and, therefore, must be rejected. Thus, all forms of matter were considered evil, and salvation consisted of escaping the bondage of physical substance. They taught that special knowledge existed and could only be obtained by a select elite. While proclaiming to be Christians, Gnostics denied that God became incarnate, died, and raised again; Jesus, according to the Gnostics, did not appear in the flesh but rather as a spirit who looked as if He was in a human body.

    Montanus, a self-proclaimed Christian prophet, began teaching that he was the mouthpiece of the Holy Spirit. He felt that spirituality was decreasing in the Church, so he started his own congregations. He placed great emphasis on signs and wonders. He was excommunicated from the Church, and the sect he founded, Montanism, was condemned heretical.

    2nd Century

  • Ignatius of Antioch Martyred

    The Apostolic Father Ignatius of Antioch is martyred in Rome.

    110 or 115 AD

  • Death of Trajan

    Emperor Trajan dies.

    117 AD

  • Hadrian Becomes Emperor

    Hadrian becomes the emperor. He is as cruel as his predecessor. It is estimated that as many as 10,000 Christians were killed during his reign.

    117 AD

  • Birth of St. Irenaeus of Lyons

    Saint Irenaeus of Lyons is born near Smyrna. St. Irenaeus was a disciple of Polycarp. He was the first to write a sustained criticism of Gnosticism and became instrumental in refuting it. His work included five volumes “Against Heresies.” Irenaeus also developed the concept of writing an introduction to theology for the laity. He was also the first Christian thinker to deal with the doctrines of sin and redemption in depth. Irenaeus proposed the Theory of Recapitulation, which taught that Jesus was a “new head” for Christianity, just as Adam had been. Thus, all humanity is capable of being saved. Moreover, the theory emphasized the importance of Jesus’ life in its entirety, including His Incarnation, rather than His death alone. This theory became the base for Orthodox theology. Irenaeus also believed in Theosis or Divinization, which is the act of becoming more like God. He thought that the goal of history was for mankind to dwell in unity with God. In turn, his view of Satan was that Satan became jealous of this high view of humanity and thus tempted Adam and Eve. Additionally, Irenaeus believed that God’s desire for humanity would have led to Jesus’ Incarnation even if sin had not entered the world. The Fall only necessitated the Crucifixion and Resurrection of Christ. All in all, St. Irenaeus of Lyons is often regarded as the first true theologian of Christianity.

    120 AD

  • Death of Hadrian

    Emperor Hadrian dies.

    138 AD

  • Antonius Pius Becomes Emperor

    Antonius becomes the Roman emperor. He stopped all persecution against Christians.

    138 AD

  • Polycarp of Smyrna Martyred

    The Apostolic Father Polycarp of Smyrna is martyred in his own city. There is a gruesome account of his martyrdom titled “Martyrdom of Polycarp.”

    155 AD

  • Death of Justin Martyr

    Justin Martyr is martyred during persecution.

    165 AD

  • Muratorian Canon

    The Muratorian Canon is formed. It possessed a list of writings from “authoritative prophets and apostles.” The list excluded Hebrews, James, and 1 & 2 Peter. It underscored the desire for a Christian Bible.

    170 AD

  • Celsus Critiques Christianity

    Celsus, a second-century Greek philosopher, writes, “On the True Doctrine: A Discourse Against the Christians.” The work critiqued Christian beliefs and argued against their validity. Christianity was faced with a choice: respond to philosophical criticism or become another folk religion.

    175 AD

  • Birth of Origen

    Origen of Alexandria is born in Alexandria, Egypt. Origen was likely taught at the Alexandrian catechetical school and probably received instruction from Clement of Alexandria. Origen became the principal of the school at the age of 18, his genius noticed by many. He possessed a prolific writing career. He was not afraid of Greek philosophy and utilized it in defending Christianity. One of Origen’s compositions is “Contra Celsum,” a work that refutes Celsus’ refutation of Christianity. In writing this work, Origen defeated a massive threat to the credibility of Christianity. He was a speculative thinker and saw three levels of meaning in Scripture: corporeal, soulish, and spiritual. Needless to say, Origen, consistent with Alexandrian thought, embraced an allegorical interpretation of Scripture. His doctrine of God involved the view that God is perfect and cannot change. Thus, Origen believed that God didn’t change even during the Incarnation of Jesus. Nevertheless, Origen was not without criticism. He was accused of embracing heresy and being an influencer of every heresy that came after him. He was posthumously condemned in 553 AD.

    185-186 AD

  • Apostles’ Creed

    An ancient baptismal formula used in Rome becomes known as the “Apostles’ Creed” in the late second century or early third century. It was widely regarded as an authoritative summary of the Apostles’ teachings and was viewed as a criterion for membership within the Church.

    3rd Century

  • Third Century Persecution

    Emperor Septimius Severus fears civil war and commands Syncretism. Christians and Jews refused to conform, making them targets of persecution.

    Emperor Decius, perceiving a lack of unity of religion, began a holy war against those who refused to bow to the gods of Rome’s pagan past. He ordered people to make sacrifices and obtain certificates. Without a certificate, there was no protection. Some Christians obeyed the edict, some refused, some recanted under torture, and some refused even under torture. Those who survived the torture became known as “the confessors.”

    3rd Century

  • Third Century Centers

    As the Church became more structured and theology formalized, two great centers of Christian thought emerged: Carthage (West) and Alexandria (East). Those in Carthage were more focused on practicality and desired to develop a sound system of church life. Thinkers include Cyprian and Tertullian. Those in Alexandria were more interested in using Platonic philosophy to communicate the Gospel to a broad audience. They wanted to demonstrate that Christianity was the Absolute Truth that philosophers sought. Thinkers in Alexandria include Clement and Origen.

    Clement of Alexandria followed the motto, “All truth is God’s truth wherever it may be found.” He produced “Exhortation to the Heathen,” a polemic against paganism. In the composition, he suggested that some of the thoughts of Socrates and Plato came from Moses. He also wrote “The Stromata,” which was an attempt to create a comprehensive Christian philosophy. Clement appeared to embrace some Gnostic thought in that he treated the body and matter as “lower nature” in contrast to the soul. He also believed that God was passionless and without emotion, a standard for which Christians should aim.

    Tertullian eschewed Greek philosophy, fearing that it would corrupt the message of salvation. He taught a blind faith, a teaching that emphasized that all speculation outside of Divine Revelation, including apostolic writings, is useless. Tertullian fought against the heresy of Marcion, who had developed his own biblical canon that excluded anything Jewish, and against Praxeas, who developed a heresy later known as Sabellianism.

    3rd Century

  • Third Century Conclusions

    By the end of the century, churches appeared in every major center of the Roman empire. The Church flourished and was poised to become a spiritual force in the 4th century.

    3rd Century

  • Birth of St. Cyprian of Carthage

    Saint Cyprian of Carthage is born into a wealthy pagan family in Carthage. Cyprian received the best education possible and managed his family’s vast estates. He was afforded a life of luxury. However, he converted to Christianity at the age of 40 and began distributing his wealth to the poor. The love he garnered by the people led to a swift ordination.

    Cyprian faced a challenging issue within the early Church. Believers who had lapsed under Emperor Decius’ edict sought readmission to the Church. The Confessors, those who had withstood torture and remained faithful to the Church, readmitted them of their own accord. However, Cyprian believed that readmission required oversight and that it was the duty of ordained bishops to readmit apostates. Cyprian insisted that they must make proper repentance and penance. Moreover, Novatian, who later declared himself bishop, thought that none of the apostates should be readmitted.

    Cyprian’s view of the role of bishops evolved into the spiritual hierarchy of the East and West. He believed in apostolic succession and the necessity of the Church for salvation. He saw the Church as the “indispensable ark of salvation.” He also believed that the process of salvation began at baptism and that baptism may only be performed by an authorized bishop or their representative.

    200 AD

  • Death of St. Irenaeus

    Saint Irenaeus of Lyons dies during a massacre of Christians.

    202 AD

  • Decius Becomes Emperor

    Decius becomes the Roman emperor. He initiates a holy war against those refusing to bow down to the Roman gods.

    249 AD

  • Death of Emperor Decius

    Emperor Decius dies.

    251 AD

  • Birth of St. Anthony

    Saint Anthony is born. Anthony was born to a wealthy family. However, after hearing a sermon on the rich young ruler, he decided to give his wealth to the poor. He became one of the first desert monks. He was sought by the sick for healing and was known for his wisdom on contemplation and prayer.

    251 AD

  • Death of Origen

    Origen of Alexandria dies in Caesarea, Palestine.

    254-255 AD

  • Paul of Samosata Condemed

    Paul of Samosata is condemned and deposed for his teaching of Adoptionism Christology, which taught that Jesus was not eternal but instead adopted by the Father.

    268 AD

  • Birth of St. Pachomius

    Saint Pachomius is born. Pachomius was born to a pagan family and had little exposure to Christian teachings. He was later taken from his home and forced to join the army. During his time in the military, Pachomius encountered a group of Christians. Their act of love so moved him that he vowed to devote his life to serving others if allowed to leave their military. He was eventually allowed to leave. He fulfilled his vow and later became known as the main organizer of communal monasticism.

    286 AD

  • Fourth Century Developments

    In the fourth century, the Church saw a formalization of hierarchical organizational structure, the formulation of creeds, and the identification of a canon of Christian Scriptures. Persecutions against Christians also began to subside. The world sees the first “Christian” emperor in Emperor Constantine. Christianity became a part of “Roman pop-culture.”

    Athanasius composes “On the Incarnation of the Word,” stressing the importance of the Incarnation and “Against the Arians,” which was a polemic against the Arian heresy. The Cappadocian Fathers built on his work, defending Trinitarian doctrine and taught that God was one “ousia” (substance) and three hypostases (persons). They also maintained the full deity of the Son along with His distinction from the Father, refuting Arianism and Eunomianism, an extreme form of Arianism. Thinkers of this group include Basil of Caesarea, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa.

    See “Birth of Basil of Caesarea” and “Birth of Gregory of Nazianzus” for an overview of their contributions.

    Gregory of Nyssa lacked the prestigious education of the other two, yet he is likely the most accomplished theologian of the three. He took over the mantle of theological leadership after the death of Basil. He emphasized God’s mystery and incomprehensibility and defined evil as the privation of goodness. He also taught the concept of instantiation, using gold coins as an example. He promoted a way of theological thinking known as “Via Negativa,” which described God based on what He is not.

    This century also saw the rise of John Chrysostom, a popular early Church Father.

    4th Century

  • Fourth Century Heresies

    The Church in the fourth century experienced the rising of threats from within. Arius of Alexandria taught that Jesus was not eternal and was created. He developed the motto, “there was when the Son was not.” Arius studied under Lucian, who was a disciple of Paul of Samosata. Arius couldn’t openly teach adoptionism, so he developed a subtler form. He denied the absolute divinity of Jesus because he believed that Jesus’ life as a human would have brought change to God and that if Jesus was God, then God would have suffered in Him on the Cross. These two concepts were inconceivable to Arius. One key thought of Arianism is that God is by nature removed from creatureliness and that Jesus Christ must be a creature if the Logos became human in Him. Another key thought was that salvation involved freely choosing to submit to God and be joined with Him. If Jesus, as the example of such submission, was God, we cannot replicate His example. The Arian Controversy caused such an uproar among early Christianity that Emperor Constantine called a council to settle the dispute. Later, an extreme form of Arianism known as Eunomianism emerged.

    Saint Athanasius of Alexandria was a fierce opponent of Arius. He defended the deity of Christ, refusing to accept anything less than the recognition of Christ as God. He believed that any alterations to such a belief threatened salvation.

    4th Century

  • Constantine Becomes Emperor

    Constantine attacks Rome and becomes the emperor. His appeal to any god to help him led to a vision of a cross. He later made the Edict of Milan, which ended persecution for Christians. He gradually began favoring Christian over other religions and went on to see himself as a “bishop of bishops” or the “thirteenth apostle.”

    312 AD

  • Synod Banishes Arius

    A synod of 100 bishops banishes Arius from Alexandria. He was accused of teaching that the Logos could have fallen just as Satan had. He was also charged with teaching a variation of Paul of Samosata’s adoptionist Christology.

    318 AD

  • Council of Nicaea

    Emperor Constantine initiates a council to settle the Arian Controversy. All bishops are invited, of which about 318 of them attend. Various issues are dealt with, but the big issue revolved around Arius’ teaching. Eusebius of Nicomedia reads a statement denying the deity of Christ, causing an uproar. Constantine intervened to quell the crowd. Constantine, likely under advice from an advisor, recommends using the word “homoousios,” which means “one substance” or “one being” to describe Christ’s relationship with the Father. Most of the bishops agreed. The Nicene Creed was formed, effectively “putting flesh on the skeleton” of the Apostles’ Creed. Eusebius of Nicomedia and Theognis of Nicaea refused to sign the creed, making it clear that the controversy was not over. Nevertheless, Arius was deposed, condemned as a heretic, and exiled.

    325 AD

  • Birth of Basil of Caesarea

    Basil of Caesarea is born into a wealthy family. His sister, Macrina, influenced him and his brother, Gregory of Nyssa, spiritually. He attended the best school for Greek culture and philosophy in Athens, where he met Gregory of Nazianzus. He later renounced his family’s wealth, forfeited his legacy as the eldest son, and founded a monastery.

    Basil fought against the pneumatomachians, who taught that the Holy Spirit was not divine and was inferior to the Father and Son. He wrote “On the Holy Spirit,” fearing that the Holy Spirit was forgotten about amongst the debate of the person and deity of Christ.

    330 AD

  • Birth of Gregory of Nazianzus

    Gregory of Nazianzus is born around the same time as Basil of Caesarea. He was the best of the Cappadocian Fathers at putting Trinitarian theology into language that was easy to understand. He taught that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are not three independent beings or actions, but rather three interdependent relations living in community.

    Gregory was later made Patriarch of Constantinople by Emperor Theodosius, who recognized his ability. Thus, he became a presiding officer over the Council of Constantinople in 381.

    330 AD

  • Arius Restored

    Constantine reinstates Arius as a presbyter in Alexandria and demands Athanasius to comply. Arius refuses to affirm the teaching of homoousios, so Athanasius rejects him and ignores the emperor’s pleas. As a result, Athanasius is exiled to the furthest outpost of the empire.

    332 AD

  • Birth of St. Martin of Tours

    Saint Martin of Tours was an exemplary monk who later became the bishop of Tours. He was known for his sanctity and humility. It was claimed that God did many great works through him. He left such an impression on the Church that after him, people believed bishops should be like him and exhibit a monastic lifestyle as much as possible.

    335 AD

  • Death of Emperor Constantine

    Emperor Constantine dies. His son, Constantius, succeeds him and reinstates Athanasius.

    337 AD

  • Birth of St. John Chrysostom

    Saint John Chrysostom is born. John later became the bishop of Constantinople and became a hero of the poor and downtrodden masses for his rhetoric against the wealth of some Christians. He typified the East’s preference for theology rooted in worship and preaching done “in the mind of Christ.”

    347 AD

  • Birth of St. Jerome

    Saint Jerome is born. Jerome was a brilliant scholar, later becoming one of the most influential theologians of the West. He later composed the Latin Vulgate, a Latin translation of Scripture. It was his greatest contribution to the Church.

    348 AD

  • Birth of St. Augustine of Hippo

    Saint Augustine of Hippo is born in a small city in North Africa to a Christian mother and pagan father. He credits his mother for his eventual repentance because of her prayers for him.

    Augustine marked the end of the early Church and the beginning of the medieval Church theologically. He taught the absolute Sovereignty of God and the human soul’s complete dependence on Him for salvation. He was not the first to propose such an idea, but he put a new spin on it. His teaching is referred to as monergism and holds that the human soul is entirely passive in salvation and that God is the agent responsible for all history and individual salvation. This contradicted the theological belief in synergism, which held that human and divine agency cooperate to produce history and salvation. In other words, Augustine didn’t believe in free will.

    Augustine also battled Manicheanism, a type of Gnosticism that taught that the stars controlled human actions and destinies.

    Moreover, Saint Augustine battled Pelagius, who taught that humans could choose to live righteously and move toward God before being seized by God’s transforming grace. Augustine held to the view that an individual must be seized by grace to change and obtain salvation. His position led to the formation of his concept of predestination. He also formulated the doctrine of Original sin, which highlighted that sin exists within an individual, as can be seen by selfishness in infants.

    354 AD

  • Death of St. Anthony

    Saint Anthony dies and instructs his disciples to keep the place of his burial a secret.

    356 AD

  • Easter Letter of Athanasius

    Athanasius, Bishop of Alexandria, sends out a letter to congregations in Egypt. The letter listed the canon of New Testament books that we have today. The New Testament canon was officially settled in 393 and 397 in Hippo and Carthage.

    367 AD

  • Basil of Caesarea Becomes Archbishop

    Basil of Caesarea becomes the archbishop of Caesarea, a very influential center in the Christian East.

    370 AD

  • Death of St. Athanasius

    Saint Athanasius dies.

    373 AD

  • Basil Writes “On the Holy Spirit”

    Basil of Caesarea composes, “On the Holy Spirit.”

    375 AD

  • Death of Basil of Caesarea

    Basil of Caesarea dies.

    379 AD

  • Council of Constantinople

    The Council of Constantinople is called by Emperor Theodosius I. The Nicene Creed is reaffirmed with additions, including references to the Holy Spirit and Apostolic Church. Trinitarian theology became the universal belief of all Christians as a result of the council.

    381 AD

  • Death of Gregory of Nazianzus

    Gregory of Nazianzus dies.

    391 AD

  • Death of St. Martin of Tours

    Saint Martin of Tours dies, leaving behind an influential legacy.

    397 AD

  • Issues in the Fifth Century

    After the issue of the deity of Christ was settled, a new problem emerged. If God is eternal and cannot change, how did He pull off the Incarnation? Two competing camps emerged. While both camps presupposed God’s immutability and impassibility, they emphasized different aspects of the Incarnation. Alexandria, concerned with true divinity touching humanity to heal it, proposed that the Logos replaced the rational human will in Jesus. The result was a human Logos without a full human will. In the fourth century, Apollinarius, carrying to its conclusion the theology of former Alexandrian heroes, developed a view that can be described as “God in a bod.” Eutyches taught that the Logos absorbed the human nature of Christ. Additionally, Monophysitism taught that Christ had only one nature. They were unwilling to accept an impersonal human nature of Christ. Antioch, in contrast, held that Jesus had a full human will. For Christians to become like Christ, according to Antioch, Jesus had to have a human will. Moreover, Christ had to possess a human will in order to redeem it. Otherwise, Jesus could not redeem the fallen nature of mankind. Theodore Of Mopsuestia taught a dualistic Christology. He taught that the Logos could not be in a natural union with the human body of Jesus Christ. Thus, the Son of God was in a union with a human body in which the human changed, but the Son of God did not. However, the humanity of Jesus was necessary because, without it, Jesus’ union with God would be impossible for Christians to replicate. His union would be static and automatic rather than through free will. Theodore’s dualistic Christology embraced languaged that suggested adoptionism. He taught that the human person Jesus and the Son of God cooperated so closely together that it was impossible to separate their activity. Nestorius taught that the Logos indwelt the person of Jesus. Thus, the union was merely mechanical rather than organic. The debate was complex and, as can be predicted, led to councils and synods to resolve the issue.

    5th Century

  • Second Exile of St. John Chrysostom

    Saint John Chrysostom is exiled a second time. It was practically a death sentence at his age.

    406 AD

  • Fall of Rome

    Rome falls to the Goth.

    410 AD

  • Council of Ephesus

    Cyril and Leo I of the Alexandrian camp oppose Nestorius and John of Antioch of the Antiochene camp. The Antiochene representatives were delayed because of the weather. However, Cyril convened the council anyway and condemned and excommunicated Nestorius. John of Antioch arrived, assembled his own meeting, and upheld the teachings of Nestorius while excommunicating Cyril. Finally, representatives of Leo I from Rome arrive, uphold Cyril’s decision and excommunicate John. Emperor Theodosius II responds by arresting Cyril and John and forces them to compromise by affirming that Jesus was “one person with two natures.”

    431 AD

  • Death of Cyril

    Cyril dies and is replaced by Dioscorus of Alexandria.

    444 AD

  • Council of Ephesus II (or Robber’s Synod)

    A monk named Eutyches claims that Jesus was one substance with the Father but not with humanity. Dioscorus of Alexandria possessed the backing of the emperor and prevented representatives from the Antiochene camp from speaking. Patriarch Flavian tried to speak but was beaten to death by Dioscorus’ supporters. Leo I became angry and named the council “Robber’s Synod.”

    449 AD

  • Council of Chalcedon

    The Council of Chalcedon saw the merger of the Alexandrian emphasis on the one Person of Christ and Antioch’s emphasis on the two natures of Christ. The result was the view that Jesus was one Person with two natures. The council concluded that Jesus is “complete in Godhood and complete in manhood, truly God and truly man, . . . in two natures, without confusion, without change, without division or without separation, . . . coming together to form one person.” The council, in effect, created fences around the hypostatic union known as the “four fences.”

    Furthermore, Patriarchs of Rome and Constantinople were given jurisdiction over West-East, respectively. It was decided that the pope was first among equals. Additionally, the council concluded that to be a Christian, one must embrace the conclusions of the councils of Nicaea, Constantinople, and Chalcedon. The emperor also decreed that no further controversies over matters of faith were allowed.

    Moreover, all participants of Robber’s Synod were forgiven except for Dioscorus and Eutyches. The Arian issue is settled once and for all.

    451 AD

  • Sixth Century Issues

    Although the debate was “settled” at the Council of Chalcedon, the issue regarding the natures of Christ surfaced again. Timothy Aelurus, Bishop of Alexandria, declared anathema against the Chalcedonian definition. Alexandria began to anathemize those who taught that Christ had two natures. This sparked great controversy because the prospect of the church at Alexandria, a group, could not be allowed.

    6th Century

  • Justinian the Great Becomes Emperor

    Justian the Great becomes the emperor. Like Constantine, he meddled in Church affairs. He chose Leonitas of Byzantium to preside over the Council of Constantinople II.

    527 AD

  • Synod of Orange

    A semi-Augustinian view becomes the Church’s official doctrine, preserving the dependency of God’s grace for salvation and the necessity of human response.

    529 AD

  • Birth of Pope Gregory the Great

    Pope Gregory the Great (or Gregory I) is born in Rome. Gregory became the main catalyst to the Western Church becoming Roman Catholic. He is regarded as one of the four great doctors of the Church by Roman Catholic tradition alongside Ambrose, Jerome, and Augustine. Gregory saw his role as bishop as authoritative over other bishops and rejected the notion that he was “first among equals.”

    The East reacted to Gregory’s claim of the superiority of the Bishop of Rome and his innovations in Christian teaching and practice. In response, the West and East rarely read each other and grew distant. They began to act as if the other did not exist. The East began to slowly become “Eastern Orthodox.” Each began to deal with various problems in different ways.

    540 AD

  • Council of Constantinople II

    Leonitas of Byzantium proposes a third solution to the issue of the Incarnation of Christ. He suggested that two things can be so united that their distinct natures subsist within a single hypostasis (person). This meant that the two interpenetrate without forming a third thing because they are held together by the hypostasis or subsistence of one. A nature needs a person to be complete, but two natures can share one person. The council accepted Leonitas’ solution, and it became orthodox.

    553 AD

  • Gregory I Becomes Bishop of Rome

    Gregory I becomes the Bishop of Rome. He sees his office as authoritative and rejects the notion that he is “first among equals.” Gregory became the main catalyst of the West’s evolution to Roman Catholicism.

    590 AD

  • Death of Maximus the Confessor

    Maximus the Confessor is tortured to death after openly denouncing the emperor’s influence over Church and theology. Maximus refused to accept Monothelitism, which held that although Jesus had two natures and was one person, He only had one will. If such were true, it would have certainly been His divine will. However, if Jesus did not possess a human will, then He could not have healed it.

    655 AD

  • Council of Constantinople III

    Monophysitism is completely defeated and Monothelitism is rejected.

    680-681 AD

  • Seventh Ecumenical Council

    Bishops gathered and condemned the iconoclasts. The council concluded that holy images (icons) were not idols and were to be venerated. It was held that there was a difference between worship and veneration. This settled the dispute between John of Damascus and Iconoclasm.

    787 AD

  • Synod of Bishops in Germany

    A synod of bishops meet in Germany and declare those who do not accept the addition of “and the Son” in the Nicene Creed to be heretical. The pope rejected the extreme measure, seeking to prevent a schism between the East and West. However, the damage was done, and the Church was one step closer to the Great Schism in 1054.

    809 AD

  • Eleventh Century Developments

    The unified but distinct Church officially becomes divided, leading to the official creation of the Roman Catholic Church and the Eastern Orthodox Church because of a papal bull excommunicating the patriarch of Constantinople and his associates.

    11th Century

  • The Great Schism

    A papal bull is delivered to the Great Church of the Holy Wisdom, excommunicating the patriarch of Constantinople and his associates. The culmination of differences, a lack of communication, and too much institutionalism rather than movement divided Christendom. The Church has never been unified since. While cultural differences most likely played a factor in the separation, both sides point to papal authority, the authoritative status of the bishop of Rome, and the issue of Filioque, the addition of “and the Son” into the Nicene Creed.

    1054 AD

Sources: Class Presentations, "The Story of Christian Theology" by Roger E. Olson, "ESV Global Study Bible," and Encyclopædia Britannica.
